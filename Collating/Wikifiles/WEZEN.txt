'''Delta Canis Majoris''' (δ CMa, δ Canis Majoris) is a [[star]] in the [[constellation]] [[Canis Major]]. It has the traditional name '''Wezen''' or '''Wesen'''. It is a yellow-white [[stellar classification|F-type]] [[supergiant]] with an [[apparent magnitude]] of +1.83. Since 1943, the [[spectrum]] of this star has served as one of the stable anchor points by which other stars are classified.<ref name=baas25_1319/>

==Visibility==
δ Canis Majoris is  the third brightest star in the constellation after [[Sirius]] and [[Adhara]], with an [[apparent magnitude]] of +1.83, and is a white or yellow-white in colour. Lying about 10 degrees south southeast of Sirius, it only rises to about 11 degrees above the horizon at the latitude of the United Kingdom.<ref>{{cite book|last=Mobberly|first=Martin|title=The Caldwell Objects and How to Observe Them|url=http://books.google.com/?id=amPrWoOWgHcC&pg=PA136&dq=Wezen+Canis#v=onepage&q=Wezen%20Canis&f=false|accessdate=28 May 2010|year=2009|publisher=Springer|isbn=1-4419-0325-9|page=136}}</ref> The open cluster [[NGC 2354]] is located only 1.3 degrees east of Delta Canis Majoris.<ref>http://www.angelfire.com/ky/astronomers/Canis.html</ref> As with the rest of Canis Major, Wezen is most visible in winter skies in the northern hemisphere, and summer skies in the southern. It theoretically makes up the Great Dog's hind quarter.<ref name="allen"/>

==History and naming==
The traditional name, Wezen, is derived from the medieval Arabic '''وزن''' ''al-wazn'', which means "weight" in modern Arabic. The name was for one of a pair of stars, the other being Hadar, which has now come to refer to [[Beta Centauri]]. It is unclear whether the pair of stars was originally [[Alpha Centauri|Alpha]] and Beta Centauri or [[Alpha Columbae|Alpha]] and [[Beta Columbae]]. In any case, the name was somehow applied to both Delta Canis Majoris and Beta Columbae.<ref>{{cite book |author= Kunitzsch, Paul; Smart, Tim|year = 2006|title = A Dictionary of Modern star Names: A Short Guide to 254 Star Names and Their Derivations |edition = 2nd rev.  |publisher = [[New Track Media#Sky Publishing|Sky Pub]] |location = [[Cambridge, Massachusetts|Cambridge]], [[Massachusetts|MA]] |isbn = 978-1-931559-44-7 |page = 23}}</ref>  [[Richard Hinckley Allen]] muses that the name alludes to the difficulty the star has rising above the (northern hemisphere) horizon.<ref name="allen">{{cite book|author= Allen R H|year = 1963 |origyear=1899|title = [[Star Names: Their Lore and Meaning]] |edition = [[Reprint|rep]]. |publisher = [[Dover Publications]] Inc. |location = [[New York City|New York]], [[New York|NY]]|isbn = 0-486-21079-0 |page = 130|authorlink = Richard Hinckley Allen}}</ref> Astronomer [[James B. Kaler|Jim Kaler]] has noted the aptness of the traditional name given the star's massive nature.<ref name="Kaler">{{cite web|url=http://stars.astro.illinois.edu/sow/wezen.html|title=Wezen|last=Kaler|first=James B.|work=Stars website|publisher=University of Illinois|accessdate=28 May 2010}}</ref>

In [[Chinese language|Chinese]], {{lang|zh|弧矢}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Hú Shǐ}}), meaning ''[[Well (Chinese constellation)|Bow and Arrow]]'',<ref>弧矢 (Hú Shǐ) is westernized into '''Koo She'''. R.H. Allen had opinion that ''Koo She'' refers to the asterism including [[Delta Velorum|δ Velorum]] and [[Omega Velorum|ω Velorum]]. AEEA opinion is, δ Velorum is member of {{lang|zh|天社}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Tiān Shè}}), meaning ''[[Well (Chinese constellation)|Celestial Earth God's Temple]]'' asterism and ω Velorum is not member of any asterisms. 天社 (Tiān Shè) is westernized into '''Tseen She''' and R.H.Allen used the term ''Tseen She'' for Chinese name of [[Eta Carinae|η Carinae]]. See [http://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Gazetteer/Topics/astronomy/_Texts/secondary/ALLSTA/Argo_Navis*.html Richard Hinckley Allen: Star Names — Their Lore and Meaning: Argo Navis] and {{zh icon}} [http://aeea.nmns.edu.tw/2006/0607/ap060717.html AEEA (Activities of Exhibition and Education in Astronomy) 天文教育資訊網 2006 年 7 月 17 日].</ref> refers to an asterism consisting of δ Canis Majoris, [[Eta Canis Majoris|η Canis Majoris]], [[HD 63032]], [[HD 65456]], [[Omicron Puppis|ο Puppis]], [[k Puppis]], [[Epsilon Canis Majoris|ε Canis Majoris]], [[Kappa Canis Majoris|κ Canis Majoris]] and [[Pi Puppis|π Puppis]]. Consequently, δ Canis Majoris itself is known as {{lang|zh|弧矢一}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Hú Shǐ yī}}, {{lang-en|the First Star of Bow and Arrow}}.)<ref>{{zh icon}} [http://aeea.nmns.edu.tw/2006/0607/ap060717.html AEEA (Activities of Exhibition and Education in Astronomy) 天文教育資訊網 2006 年 7 月 17 日]</ref>

In the catalogue of stars in the ''Calendarium of Al Achsasi Al Mouakket'', this star was designated '''Thalath al Adzari''' ('''تالت ألعذاري''' - ''taalit al-aðārii''), which was translated into [[Latin]] as '''Tertia Virginum''', meaning ''the third virgin''.<ref>{{cite journal

 | journal=Monthly Notices of the Royal Astronomical Society
 | volume=55 | page=429 | month=June | year=1895
 | bibcode=1895MNRAS..55..429K }}</ref> This star, along with [[Epsilon Canis Majoris|ε CMa]] (Adhara), [[Eta Canis Majoris|η CMa]] (Aludra) and [[Omicron2 Canis Majoris|ο<sup>2</sup> CMa]] (Thanih al Adzari), were '''Al ʽAdhārā''' ('''ألعذاري'''), the Virgins.<ref>{{cite book
 | last=Allen | first=R. H. | year=1963
 | authorlink=Richard Hinckley Allen
 | title=Star Names: Their Lore and Meaning
 | url=http://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Gazetteer/Topics/astronomy/_Texts/secondary/ALLSTA/Canis_Major*.html
 | accessdate=2010-12-12 | edition=Reprint
 | publisher=Dover Publications Inc
 | location=New York | isbn=0-486-21079-0 | page=130 }}</ref><ref>[[Epsilon Canis Majoris|ε CMa]] as '''Aoul al Adzari''' or '''Prima Virginum''' (the first virgin), [[Omicron2 Canis Majoris|ο<sup>2</sup> CMa]] as '''Thanih al Adzari''' or '''Secunda Virginum''' (the second virgin) and δ CMa as '''Thalath al Adzari''' or '''Tertia Virginum'''  (the third virgin). [[Eta Canis Majoris|η CMa]] should be '''Rabah al Adzari''' or '''Quarta Virginum''' (the fourth virgin) consistently, but it was given by the name '''Aludra''', meaning ''the virgin'' (same meaning with Adhara ([[Epsilon Canis Majoris|ε CMa]]) or ''Al ʽAdhārā'')</ref>

==Properties==
Wezen is a [[supergiant]] of class  [[stellar classification|F8]] with a radius around 237 times that of the Sun. Its surface temperature is around 5,818&nbsp;K,<ref>{{cite journal|last= Davis J, Booth AJ, Ireland MJ, Jacob AP, North JR, Owens SM, Robertson JG, Tango WJ, Tuthill PG|year=2007|title=The Emergent Flux and Effective Temperature of Delta Canis Majoris|doi=10.1071/AS07017|arxiv=0709.3873|accessdate=28 May 2010|bibcode = 2007PASA...24..151D|first1= J.|last2= Booth|first2= A. J.|last3= Ireland|first3= M. J.|last4= Jacob|first4= A. P.|last5= North|first5= J. R.|last6= Owens|first6= S. M.|last7= Robertson|first7= J. G.|last8= Tango|first8= W. J.|last9= Tuthill|first9= P. G.|journal= Publications of the Astronomical Society of Australia|volume= 24|issue= 3|pages= 151 }}</ref> and it weighs around 17 solar masses. Its absolute magnitude is –6.87,<ref name=rmaa39_3/> and it lies around 1,600 light years away. It is rotating at a speed of around 28&nbsp;km/s, and hence may take a year to rotate fully. Only around 10 million years old, Wezen has stopped fusing hydrogen in its core. Its outer envelope is beginning to expand and cool, and in the next 100,000 years it will become a red supergiant as its core fuses heavier and heavier elements. Once it has a core of iron, it will collapse and explode as a [[supernova]].<ref name="Kaler"/>

If Wezen were as close to Earth as Sirius is, it would be as bright as a half-full moon.<ref>{{cite book|last1=Ridpath|first1=Ian|last2=Tirion|first2=Will|title=The Monthly Sky Guide|url=http://books.google.com/?id=UFQIutJ8GSsC&pg=PA15&dq=%22Delta+Canis+Majoris%22#v=onepage&q=%22Delta%20Canis%20Majoris%22&f=false|year=2003|publisher=Cambridge University Press|isbn=0-521-53306-6|page=16}}</ref>
