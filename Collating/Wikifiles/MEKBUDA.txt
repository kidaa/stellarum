'''Zeta Geminorum''' (ζ Gem, ζ Geminorum) is the [[Bayer designation]] for a [[star]] in the [[zodiac]] [[constellation]] [[Gemini (constellation)|Gemini]], on the outstretched left 'leg' of the twin [[Pollux (star)|Pollux]]. It has the traditional name '''Mekbuda''', meaning ''the lion's folded paw''. As a member of the category of [[variable star]]s known as [[classical Cepheid variable|classical Cepheid]]s, it has a regular pulsation frequency that is determined by its mass. Because the mass allows the luminosity to be directly determined, the star serves as an important calibrator for the [[cosmic distance ladder]].<ref name=benedict2007/><ref name=vanleeuwen07/><ref name=majaess2012/>

==Observation history==
In 1825, German astronomer [[Johann Friedrich Julius Schmidt|Julius Schmidt]] discovered that this star varies in brightness with a period of about 10 days,<ref name=allen1899/> although it had been suspected of variability as early as 1790.<ref name=pdoo9_105/> In 1899, American astronomer [[William Wallace Campbell|W. W. Campbell]] announced that Zeta Geminorum has a variable [[radial velocity]].<ref name=apj9_86/> (This variation was independently discovered by Russian astronomer [[Aristarkh Belopolsky]], published in 1901.)<ref name=pdoo9_105/> Based on his observations, Campbell later published [[orbital elements]] for the binary. However, he found that the curve departed from a [[keplerian orbit]] and even suggested that it was a triple star system in order to explain the irregularities.<ref name=apj13_90/> The periodic variation in radial velocity was subsequently explained as the result of radial pulsations that occur in a class of variable stars known as [[Cepheid variable]]s—named after [[Delta Cephei]].<ref name=pdoo9_105/>

The periodicity of the star is itself variable, a trend first noted by German astronomer [[Paul Guthnick]] in 1920, who suspected that the period change was the result of an orbiting companion. In 1930, Danish astronomer [[Axel Nielsen]] suggested that the change was instead the result in a steady decrease of about 3.6 seconds per year in the period.<ref name=apj188_L75/>

==Properties==
This star is a [[Classical Cepheid variable]] that undergoes regular, periodic variation in luminosity because of radial pulsations. In the [[V band]], the [[apparent magnitude]] varies between a high of 3.68 and a low of 4.16 (with a mean of 3.93) over a period of 10.148 days.<ref name=aaa504_3_959/> This period of variation is decreasing at the rate of 3.1 seconds per year, or 0.085 seconds per cycle.<ref name=apj188_L75/> It has a nominal [[stellar classification]] of G0&nbsp;Ibv,<ref name=SIMBAD/> with the [[luminosity class]] of Ib indicating that this is an intermediate luminous [[supergiant]]. The 'v' suffix means that it has variable spectral features, with the actual classification changing between F7Ib and G3Ib over the course of a pulsation cycle. Likewise the [[effective temperature]] of the outer envelope varies between 5,780&nbsp;K and 5,260&nbsp;K,<ref name=aaa367_876/> while the radius varies from 61 to 69 times the [[solar radius|Sun's radius]].<ref name=aaa474_3_975/> On average, it is radiating about 2,900 times the luminosity of the Sun.<ref name=aaa352_495/>

There is no evidence of a stellar companion to Zeta Geminorum.<ref name=apj188_L75/> However, the star was recently discovered to belong to a [[star cluster]].<ref name=majaess2012/> [[Delta Cephei]], the prototypical [[classical Cepheid variables]], is also a member of a [[star cluster]].<ref name=dezeeuw1999/><ref name=majaess2012b/> The Cepheid's cluster membership, along with recent [[Hubble Space Telescope]] and [[Hipparcos]] [[parallax]]es, place strong constraints on the star's distance: {{nowrap|363 ± 9(σ<sub>2</sub>) ± 26(σ)}} [[parsec]]s.<ref name=majaess2012/> Zeta Geminorum is thus an important calibrator for establishing the [[cosmic distance ladder]].

==Etymology==
This star has the traditional name '''Mekbuda''', which comes from ancient Arabic where it and the star Mebsuta ([[Epsilon Geminorum]]) were the paws of a lion. Mekbuda comes from a phrase meaning ''the lion's folded paw''.<ref name=allen1899/> In [[Chinese language|Chinese]], {{lang|zh|井宿}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Jǐng Su}}), meaning ''[[Well (Chinese constellation)|Well]] (asterism)'', refers to an asterism consisting of ζ Geminorum,  [[Mu Geminorum|μ Geminorum]], [[Gamma Geminorum|γ Geminorum]], [[Nu Geminorum|ν Geminorum]], [[Xi Geminorum|ξ Geminorum]], [[Epsilon Geminorum|ε Geminorum]], [[36 Geminorum]] and [[Lambda Geminorum|λ Geminorum]].<ref>{{zh icon}} ''中國星座神話'', written by 陳久金. Published by 台灣書房出版有限公司, 2005, ISBN 978-986-7332-25-7.</ref> Consequently, ζ Geminorum itself is known as {{lang|zh|井宿七}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Jǐng Su qī}}, {{lang-en|the Seventh Star of Well}}.)<ref>{{zh icon}} [http://www.lcsd.gov.hk/CE/Museum/Space/Research/StarName/c_research_chinengstars_m.htm 香港太空館 - 研究資源 - 亮星中英對照表], Hong Kong Space Museum. Accessed on line November 23, 2010.</ref>
