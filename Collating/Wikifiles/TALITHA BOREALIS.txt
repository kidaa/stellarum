'''Iota Ursae Majoris''' ('''Iota&nbsp;UMa''', '''ι&nbsp;Ursae Majoris''', '''ι&nbsp;UMa''') is a [[star system]] in the northern [[circumpolar constellation]] of [[Ursa Major]]. It has an [[apparent visual magnitude]] of 3.14,<ref name=clpl4/> making it visible to the naked eye and placing it among the brighter members of this constellation. Based upon [[parallax]] measurements, it is located at a distance of {{Convert|47.3|ly|pc|abbr=off|lk=on}} from Earth. It has the traditional names '''Talitha''', '''Talitha Borealis''' and '''Alphikra Borealis''', and was also dubbed '''Dnoces''' ("Second," backwards) after [[Edward Higgins White|Edward H. White II]], an [[Apollo 1]] astronaut. The name was invented by his fellow astronaut [[Gus Grissom]] as a practical joke.<ref name=apollo15/>

The Iota Ursae Majoris system is composed of two [[binary star]]s. The brightest component is a white [[stellar classification|A-type]] [[subgiant]]. It is a [[spectroscopic binary]] whose components have an orbital period of 4028 days. The companion binary is composed of the 9th magnitude and 10th magnitude stars. These two stars orbit around each other with a period of 39.7 years, and are separated by roughly 0.7 [[arcsecond]]s, or at least 10 [[Astronomical Unit|AU]]. This pair may be the source of the X-ray emission detected from this system.<ref name=mnras415_1_854/>

The two binary systems orbit around each other once every 818 years. The apparent separation between the two binaries is rapidly decreasing as they follow their orbits. In 1841 when the B component was first discovered, they had a separation of 10.7 [[arcsecond]]s, or at least 156 AU. By 1971 their separation had decreased to 4.5 [[arcsecond]]s, or at least 66 AU. This system appears to be dynamically unstable with a high likelihood and may become disrupted on a time scale [[on the order of]] 10<sup>5</sup>&nbsp;years.<ref name=ar50_1_62/>

==Name and etymology==
*The traditional name ''Talitha''' (share with [[Kappa Ursae Majoris|κ UMa]]) comes from the Arabic phrase  {{transl|ar|''Al Fiḳrah al Thalitha''}} "the third spring, or leap, of the ghazal".<ref name=rhallen1899>[http://penelope.uchicago.edu/Thayer/E/Gazetteer/Topics/astronomy/_Texts/secondary/ALLSTA/Ursa_Major*.html Richard Hinckley Allen :Star Names — Their Lore and Meaning - Ursa Major, the Greater Bear]</ref> The term ''Borealis'' meaning "the north side" in [[Latin]].
*In [[Chinese language|Chinese]], {{lang|zh|三台}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Sān Tái}}), meaning ''[[Purple Forbidden enclosure|Three Steps]]'', refers to an asterism consisting of ι Ursae Majoris, [[Kappa Ursae Majoris|κ Ursae Majoris]], [[Lambda Ursae Majoris|λ Ursae Majoris]], [[Mu Ursae Majoris|μ Ursae Majoris]], [[Nu Ursae Majoris|ν Ursae Majoris]] and [[Xi Ursae Majoris|ξ Ursae Majoris]]. Consequently, ι Ursae Majoris itself is known as {{lang|zh|三台一}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Sān Tái yī}}, {{lang-en|the First Star of Three Steps}}) and {{lang|zh|上台一}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Shàng Tái yī}}, {{lang-en|Star of First Upper Step}}).<ref>{{zh icon}} [http://aeea.nmns.edu.tw/2006/0606/ap060621.html (Activities of Exhibition and Education in Astronomy) 天文教育資訊網 2006 年 6 月 21 日]</ref>
