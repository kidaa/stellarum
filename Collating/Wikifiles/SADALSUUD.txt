'''Beta Aquarii''' (β Aqr, β Aquarii) is a [[double star]] in the [[constellation]] [[Aquarius (constellation)|Aquarius]]. It has the traditional name '''Sadalsuud''', from an [[Arabic language|Arabic]] expression سعد السعود ''sa‘d al-su‘ūd'', the "luck of lucks". Other spellings that are sometimes encountered are '''Sad es Saud''', '''Sadalsund''', and '''Saad el Sund'''.

In the catalogue of stars in the ''Calendarium of Al Achsasi Al Mouakket'', this star was designated '''Nir Saad al Saaoud''', which was translated into [[Latin]] as '''Lucida Fortunæ Fortunarum''' (rather identic with R.H. Allen), meaning ''the brightest of luck of lucks''.<ref name=mnras55_429/>

== Properties ==

Sadalsuud is the brightest star in Aquarius with an [[apparent magnitude]] of 2.87<ref name=clpl4_99/> and a [[stellar classification]] of G0&nbsp;Ib.<ref name=araa11_29/> Since 1943, the [[spectrum]] of this star has served as one of the stable anchor points by which other stars are classified.<ref name=baas25_1319/> Based upon [[parallax]] measurements, it is located at a distance of about 540&nbsp;[[light-year]]s (160&nbsp;[[parsec]]s) from the Earth.<ref name=aaa474_2_653/> The mass of this star is about 6.0 to 6.5 times the [[solar mass|mass of the Sun]], but it is emitting roughly 2,300 times the [[solar luminosity|Sun's luminosity]] implying a radius that is 50 times that of the Sun.<ref name=aaa449_2_655/><ref name="kaler" /> It has an estimated age of 60 million years;<ref name=apj627_1_L53/> old enough for a star of this mass to evolve into a [[supergiant]]. The estimated [[effective temperature]] of the star's outer envelope is about 5,700&nbsp;K,<ref name=aaa449_2_655/> giving it the characteristic yellow hue of G-type stars.<ref name=csiro/>

X-ray emissions from the [[corona]] of this star have been detected using the [[Chandra X-ray Observatory]]; among the first such detections of X-rays for a G-type supergiant. A secondary X-ray source discovered near Beta Aquarii probably has an extragalactic origin.<ref name=apj627_1_L53/> This star belongs to a group of three intermediate mass stars with a [[space velocity (astronomy)|space velocity]] that is carrying them perpendicular to the plane of the galaxy. The other members of this grouping are [[Alpha Aquarii]] and [[Eta Pegasi]].<ref name=apj627_1_L53/>

== Mythology and etymology==

Sadalsuud, when translated, means the ''"Luckiest of the Lucky"'' or the ''"luck of lucks"''. In the context of older  [[Mythology|worldviews]] (i.e.[[Ancient Egyptian religion|Egyptian]], [[Persian mythology|Persian]] and [[Islamic mythology|Islamic]] mythology), Sadalsuud relates to the rising of the Sun when winter has passed [[Calendar#Currently used calendars|(March)]] and the season of gentle, continuous rain has begun.<ref name="Allen"/> Hence the [[Mythology|myth]] of ''"luck"'' or ''"good fortune"'' was seen as closely aligned with the essence of spring itself, the burgeoning of new life, and by extension agriculture, which in all societies is the very foundation of prosperity or ''"good fortune"''.  This mythological view of ''"the luck of the lucks"'' also belongs to the 22d [[Manzil]] [[Islamic calendar|(Arabic Lunar Mansion)]], which included the two stars [[Xi Aquarii|Bunda]] (ξ Aqr) and [[46 Capricorni|c Cap]].<ref name="Allen" /><ref>β Aqr as '''Nir Saad al Saaoud''' or '''Lucida Fortunæ Fortunarum''' (the brightest of luck of lucks) and [[Xi Aquarii|ξ Aqr]] as '''Thanih Saad al Saaoud''' or '''Secunda Fortunæ Fortunarum'''  (the second of luck of lucks). [[46 Capricorni|c Cap]] should be '''Thalath Fortunæ Fortunarum''' or '''Tertia Fortunæ Fortunarum''' (the third of luck of lucks) consistently, but Al Achsasi Al Mouakket was not designated the title for this star with uncleared consideration. Possibly according to the differences of opinion with R.H.Allen</ref>

[[Image:Aquarius et Capricornus - Mercator.jpeg|thumb|left|300px|One of the many illustrations<ref name="harvard"/> from [[Gerardus Mercator]] - in this case of Aquarius, the water-bearer, overlooking Capricorn (the mountain-goat) and pouring water into the mouth of the southern fish (Pisces).  Sadalsuud is the star in the left shoulder, just above [[Delta Capricorni|Deneb Algedi]] (δ Cap)]]

β and ξ Aquarii also constitute the Persian [[Iranian calendars|lunar mansion]] Bunda and the similar [[Copts|Coptic]] mansion Upuineuti, the meaning of which is ''"the Foundation"''.  

In Chinese mythology, β Aqr alone marks the sieu (Chinese Lunar Mansion) Heu, Hiu, or Hü, ''"the Void"'', anciently Ko, the central one of the seven sieu which, taken together, were known as Heung Wu, the Black Warrior, in the northern quarter of the sky.<ref name="Allen" />  As such, Sadalsuud is an expression of the feminine [[archetype]], the [[Yin and yang|Yin]] or ''"Void"'' (Cosmic Mother), from which, many cultures have believed, [[Big Bang|creation itself]] (birth) emanates. 

In Chinese, {{lang|zh|虚宿}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Xū Sù}}), meaning ''[[Emptiness (Chinese constellation)|Emptiness]] (asterism)'', refers to an asterism consisting of β Aquarii and [[Alpha Equulei|α Equulei]].<ref>{{zh icon}} ''中國星座神話'', written by 陳久金. Published by 台灣書房出版有限公司, 2005, ISBN 978-986-7332-25-7.</ref> Consequently, β Aquarii itself is known as {{lang|zh|虛宿一}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Xū Sù yī}}, {{lang-en|the First Star of Emptiness}}.)<ref>{{zh icon}} [http://www.lcsd.gov.hk/CE/Museum/Space/Research/StarName/c_research_chinengstars_s.htm 香港太空館 - 研究資源 - 亮星中英對照表], Hong Kong Space Museum. Accessed on line November 23, 2010.</ref>

Sadalsuud is found in Hindu texts as Kalpeny and in the context of the ancient Indian system of astronomy, [[Vedanga|Jyotisha Veda]], is located in the 23rd [[Nakshatra]] [[Nakshatra#Nakshatra descriptions|Shravishthā]], a lunar mansion which is ruled by Eight [[vasu]]s - the ''"deities of earthly abundance"'' . On the Euphrates, Sadalsuud was known as Kakkab Nammaχ, the Star of Mighty Destiny; that may have given origin to the title of the manzil, as well as to the astrologers' name for it — Fortuna Fortunarum.<ref name="Allen" />

"Sadalsuud" appears to be a single star to the naked eye, but when viewed with a [[telescope]] is seen to have two faint optical companions. The first has an [[apparent magnitude]] of 11.0. In 1947, the [[position angle]] was observed at 321 degrees with a separation from the Beta Aquarii of 35.4 [[arcseconds]].<ref name="VizieR">{{cite web |url=http://vizier.u-strasbg.fr/viz-bin/VizieR-S?CCDM%20J21316-0534A | title =CCDM (Catalog of Components of Double & Multiple stars) (Dommanget+ 2002) |work=[[VizieR]] |publisher=[[Centre de Données astronomiques de Strasbourg]] | accessdate = 2010-05-17 }}</ref><ref name="Alcyone">{{cite web |url=http://www.alcyone.de/SIT/mainstars/SIT000245.htm#Cat4 | title=Sadalsuud | publisher=Alcyone Bright Star Catalogue | accessdate=2010-05-17}}</ref> The second star has a magnitude of 11.6. Its position angle is 186 degrees with a separation from Beta Aquarii of 57.2&nbsp;[[arcseconds]].<ref name="VizieR" /><ref name="Alcyone" /> As of 2008, there is no definitive evidence that the three stars form a [[ternary star system]].<ref name=mnras389_2_869/>
