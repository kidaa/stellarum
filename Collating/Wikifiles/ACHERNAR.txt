'''Achernar''' (α Eri, α Eridani, [[alpha (letter)|Alpha]] Eridani), sometimes spelled '''Achenar''', is the brightest star in the [[constellation]] [[Eridanus (constellation)|Eridanus]] and the [[list of brightest stars|ninth-brightest star]] in the night sky. Of the top ten apparent brightest stars (excluding our Sun)—[[Sirius]], [[Canopus]], [[Alpha Centauri]], [[Arcturus]], [[Vega]], [[Capella (star)|Capella]], [[Rigel]], [[Procyon]], Achernar and [[Betelgeuse]]—Achernar is the hottest and bluest. It lies at the southern tip of the constellation.

[[File:Achernar.jpg|thumb|left|170px|Extreme rotation speed has [[Oblate spheroid|flattened]] Achernar.]]

Achernar is a bright, blue star with about six to eight [[solar mass]]es<ref name=kaler/>. As determined by the [[Hipparcos]] astrometry satellite,<ref name=aaa323_L49/><ref name=GSM/> it is approximately {{convert|139|ly|pc|lk=on}} away.<ref name=aaa474_2_653/> It is a [[main sequence]] star with a [[stellar classification]] of B6&nbsp;Vep, but is about 3,150 times more luminous than the [[Sun]]. Achernar is in the deep southern sky and never rises above the horizon beyond 33°N, roughly the latitude of [[Dallas]], Texas. Achernar is best seen from the southern hemisphere in November; it is [[Circumpolar star|circumpolar]] below 33°S.

Until about March 2000, Achernar and [[Fomalhaut]] were the two [[brightest stars|first-magnitude stars]] furthest in angular distance from any other first-magnitude star in the [[celestial sphere]]. [[Antares]], in the constellation of [[Scorpius]], is now the most isolated first-magnitude star, although Antares is located in a constellation with many bright second-magnitude stars, whereas the stars surrounding Achernar and Fomalhaut are considerably fainter. 

Infrared observations of the star using an adaptive optics system on the [[Very Large Telescope]] show that Achernar has a companion star in a close orbit. This appears to be an A-type star in the stellar classification range A0V–A3V, which suggests a stellar mass of about double the [[solar mass|Sun's mass]]. The separation of the two stars is roughly 12.3 [[Astronomical Unit|AU]] and their orbital period is at least 14–15 years.<ref name=aaa484_1_L13/>

As of 2003, Achernar is the least [[Sphere|spherical]] star in the Milky Way studied to date.<ref>See ''“Achernar the Flattest star”'' in ''‘Sky & Telescope’'' P. 20 ''‘Newsnotes’'', September 2003.</ref> It spins so rapidly that it has assumed the shape of an [[oblate spheroid]] with an equatorial diameter 56% greater than its polar diameter. The polar axis is inclined about 65° to the line of sight from the Earth.<ref name=apj676_1_L41/> Since it is actually a binary star, its highly distorted shape may cause non-negligible departures of the companion's orbital trajectory with respect to a [[Kepler orbit|Keplerian ellipse]]. A similar situation occurs for the star [[Regulus]].

Because of the distorted shape of this star, there is a significant temperature variation by latitude. At the pole, the temperature may be above 20,000&nbsp;K, while the equator is at or below 10,000&nbsp;K. The average temperature of the star is about 15,000&nbsp;K. The high polar temperatures are generating a fast polar wind that is ejecting matter from the star, creating a polar envelope of hot gas and plasma. The entire star is surrounded by an extended envelope that can be detected by its [[infrared excess|excess infrared emission]].<ref name=aaa493_3_L53/> The presence of a circumstellar disk of ionized gas is a common feature of [[Be star]]s such as this.<ref name=apj671_1_L49/>

==Name and etymology==

*The name originally comes from the Arabic '''{{lang|ar|آخر النهر}}''' ''{{transl|ar|ākhir an-nahr}}'', meaning, "The End of the River" or "River's End". 
*In [[Chinese language|Chinese]], {{lang|zh|水委}} ({{lang|zh-Latn|Shuǐ Wěi}}), meaning ''[[Chinese constellations#The Southern Asterisms (近南極星區)|Crooked Running Water]]'', refers to an asterism consisting of Achernar, [[Zeta Phoenicis|ζ Phoenicis]] and [[Eta Phoenicis|η Phoenicis]].<ref>{{zh icon}} [http://aeea.nmns.edu.tw/2006/0607/ap060727.html AEEA (Activities of Exhibition and Education in Astronomy) 天文教育資訊網 2006 年 7 月 27 日]</ref>
*The indigenous Boorong people of northwestern Victoria named it as ''Yerrerdetkurrk''.<ref name=hamacher>{{cite journal|author=Hamacher, Duane W.; Frew, David J. |year=2010|title= An Aboriginal Australian Record of the Great Eruption of Eta Carinae|journal=Journal of Astronomical History & Heritage |volume=13|issue=3|pages= 220–34|url=http://arxiv.org/ftp/arxiv/papers/1010/1010.4610.pdf}}</ref>
